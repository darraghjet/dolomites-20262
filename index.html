The Trip

Ten days walking through the most beautiful mountains in Europe

The Alta Via 1 is a north-to-south crossing of the Dolomites — a UNESCO World Heritage landscape of pale limestone towers that turn gold and pink at sunrise and sunset. You sleep in a different mountain refuge every night, each with its own character. A certified guide leads the way, all the logistics are handled, and you carry only a light daypack. Every evening there's a hot dinner, a bed, and a terrace to watch the light go down.

How a Guided Trip Works

Everything you're wondering, answered

No tents, no cooking, no route-finding, no booking headaches. Here's exactly what the days feel like.

🛏️

Where you sleep

In mountain refugios — warm, characterful huts, not campsites. A mix of cozy shared dorm rooms and, where available, private rooms. Bedding is provided (bring a light liner sheet). Many have hot showers. In Venice and the valley towns you're in comfortable 3-star+ hotels.

🍝

Meals & food

Half board is included: a proper cooked dinner and breakfast every day at the refugios — pasta, strudel, local wine, espresso on the terrace. Dinners are lively and communal. Lunch is on your own: grab a panino at a hut along the trail or carry a light picnic.

🎒

Your bags

Your main suitcase stays safely at base (Venice/Cortina) for the trek — most high huts aren't road-accessible, so nobody's luggage goes up there. You carry only a light daypack (~15 lbs) with what you need for the night. It gets reunited with you at the end.

⏰

A typical day

Relaxed mountain mornings: breakfast around 7:30, on the trail by about 8:30. You walk 4–7 hours with plenty of stops for photos, coffee and lunch, arriving at the next hut mid-afternoon with time to relax before dinner.

👥

Group & guide

A small group — usually 6 to 12 hikers — led by one certified, English-speaking mountain guide (about a 6:1 ratio). The guide handles navigation, safety, pace, local history and all the day-to-day logistics. Private, just-the-two-of-you departures are also available.

🥾

How hard is it?

Moderate — any reasonably fit hiker used to multi-day walks can do it. Days are 8–14 km with 500–1,100 m of climbing, at a comfortable pace with plenty of breaks. No via ferrata or technical climbing required — just the ability to walk 4–7 hours a day, several days in a row.

How You Pay

Simple, and low-commitment to start

You don't pay it all at once. This is how guided-tour booking typically works.

1

Reserve your spot

Put down a deposit — usually about 30% — to lock your dates and your refugio beds. This is the move that secures everything.

2

48-hour grace

Change your mind in the first 48 hours after booking and get a full refund, no questions asked.

3

Balance later

The remaining balance is due about 90 days before departure — not now. If you book inside 90 days, it's paid in full at booking.

4

Flexibility options

Most operators offer Basic / Flexible / Super-Flexible cancellation tiers at checkout, plus optional travel insurance.

Good to Know

Getting to the trail, weather, bags & food

Getting from Venice to the start

The trail begins at Lago di Braies. There's no single direct train — it's a scenic train plus a couple of buses, about 5.5–6.5 hours total. Two good routes:

RouteThe legsTime · cost
Via Cortina (most scenic)Train Venezia Santa Lucia → Calalzo di Cadore · Bus 30 → Cortina · Bus 445 → Dobbiaco · Bus 442 → Lago di Braies~5.5–6.5 h · ≈€30–50 pp
Via Fortezza (more train, less bus)Train Venezia → Fortezza/Franzensfeste (1 change) → Dobbiaco or Villabassa · Bus 442 → Lago di Braies~6–7 h · ≈€40–60 pp

Buying tickets: trains on the Trenitalia app or at any station machine (regional trains need no seat reservation, just buy same-day or ahead). Buses on the südtirolmobil / SAD app or from machines. The final leg, Bus 442, runs about every 30 minutes from Dobbiaco bus station; the first one leaves around 07:01.

⚠️ Important for a September start: from 1 July – 15 September the road to Lago di Braies is restricted. The bus to the lake (routes 439/442) must be pre-booked and pre-paid online, and the lake is only reachable 09:30–16:00 by public transport. There's a €44 combined ticket (full-day transit permit + guaranteed parking + a €22 restaurant/shop voucher). Start after 15 September and you can simply pay cash on the bus. Book at prags.bz.

Do packages cover the transfer? Guided tours usually include a transfer from a set meeting point (often Venice airport) plus trailhead transfers, and they'll sort the Braies access for you. Self-guided packages normally give detailed travel instructions and sometimes an arrival transfer as a paid add-on — otherwise you make your own way to the start. Always confirm the first-day transfer when you book.

Weather in September

September is arguably the best month on the AV1 — thinning crowds, golden light, sharp visibility and fewer thunderstorms than midsummer. But it's mountain weather: be ready for anything in a day.

☀️

Daytime

Roughly 10–18°C (50–64°F) at hiking elevations — pleasant walking in the sun, cool in shade or wind. Lower humidity than summer means crisp, clear views.

❄️

Nights & altitude

Cold once the sun drops — nights can fall below freezing above 2,000 m. The high huts (Lagazuoi, Nuvolau) are chilly, so pack warm layers and a hat even in "summer."

🌦️

Conditions

Changeable: cool sun, cloud and passing showers, with occasional heavier rain when a front comes through. A brief dusting of snow high up is possible but usually melts within a day.

📅

Best window

Early September tends to be the most stable — huts still fully open and staffed, crowds easing off. A rain shell and warm layer make almost any day workable.

Your bags — and what's in them

How many bags?

Just two, each:

  • 1 backpack (35–45L) — carried the whole trek, ~8–10 kg / 18–22 lbs. This is everything you need hut to hut.
  • 1 small duffel or case — left at your Venice/Cortina base for the city days. You don't take it on the trail (the high huts aren't road-accessible).

Huts provide blankets and pillows — you just bring a lightweight sleeping-bag liner. No tent, stove or food needed.

What goes in the pack

  • Rain shell (waterproof)
  • Light down/puffy jacket
  • Base + mid layers
  • 2–3 hiking shirts
  • Hiking pants/shorts
  • Trail runners (broken in)
  • Camp shoes/sandals
  • Hat, beanie, gloves
  • Sleeping-bag liner
  • Trekking poles
  • Headlamp
  • Battery bank + Type-L/C adapter
  • 2L water
  • Sunscreen + sunglasses
  • Blister/first-aid kit
  • Earplugs (dorms!)
  • Small towel + toiletries
  • Passport, cash (EUR), confirmations

Food & eating

🍝

Dinner & breakfast

Half board at the refugios is the highlight — a hearty multi-course dinner (soup or antipasto, pasta or polenta, a meat/veg main, dessert) and a simple breakfast. Try the local dishes: canederli (dumplings), casunziei, strudel, speck and polenta.

🥪

Lunch on the trail

Not included — and easy. Buy a panino, cake and coffee at a hut you pass (≈€8–15), or carry a light picnic. The mid-morning coffee-and-strudel stop is half the fun of the day.

🍷

Venice & the finish

In Venice, skip the tourist traps by the station and find a bàcaro for cicchetti and an ombra (small glass of wine). Save room for a proper celebration dinner in Venice or Belluno at the end.

💧

Water & drinks

Refill at huts and fountains along the way (carry ~2L). Refugios have a bar for beer, wine and coffee — bring some euros in cash, as card coverage can be patchy high up.

Who to Book With

Go direct with a local guide — or use a package

You had the right instinct: the big-name companies carry an agency markup. The AV1 is a well-marked, non-technical trail, so you can hire an independent local guide directly — or skip a full-time guide altogether — and often pay less for a more personal trip. The honest catch: a private guide for just the two of us isn't dramatically cheaper than a group package — an independent guide charges a flat ~€420/day (≈$460) for the whole party, split only two ways over ~11 days. Going direct really wins if we (a) add a couple of friends to split the guide, or (b) go self-guided/DIY, since the AV1 doesn't require a daily guide. A self-guided package

For two independent hikers this is the sweet spot. An operator books every refugio and hotel, sorts the tricky Lago di Braies access, and hands you the route on a GPS app — then you walk the trail alone, at your own pace, for roughly half the price of a guided group.
